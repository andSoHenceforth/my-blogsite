As I walked out of the exam hall this morning having just finished taking a topology mathematics exam, I thought to myself:

"Wow, that was so much different than what I have been used to...what an interesting first half of the semester this has been..."

## Allen, what is going on??

Yes, I have been away from Australia for quite some time. On the 12th of January, 2026, I arrived in Lund, Sweden, for my semester exchange programme at Lund University. I remember the day I arrived, *first time* thoughts kept trickling into my mind to form an ever-growing puddle of overthinking, for example:

- First time I am in Europe...
- First time I flew all the way to a foreign country by myself
- First time travelling... with no friends or family?

And you could say these were the *first times* I had feelings like these. 

To be honest, I think my main motivator to do a semester exchange programme was not because there was a huge desire to study here. Rather it was because I felt like:
- I so deeply wanted to set foot and travel in Europe, because I have only recognised recently that I am still a young person and I should try to at least have fun while I am young. When I will eventually have a job, it will be harder to travel so freely like this.
- To that point, I came to Sweden less for the studying aspect and more for my own personal interest; IKEA is one of my favourite stores in the entire world, ABBA is a band of the classic genre I like listening to from time to time, and I was simply interested in the Swedish language. But probably most of all, I wanted to see the Northern Lights. Oh, every time I saw a photo of the aurora online, I could only wish that one day, my eyes may be able to comprehend them in real life too. I could go on and on about my dream for seeing the lights (and spoiler alert: I have fulfilled that dream already!) but in essence I chose Sweden so I could at least be closer to seeing them in all their glory.
- I am not 100% sure what exactly I want to do in life yet. Even though I am studying mathematics and computer science, what exactly do I hope to do when I graduate in a few months time? I had hoped that this experience would help me figure that out.

In regards to that last point, I do feel that although I am still trying to figure things out, I have learned a lot of things that I would not have learned otherwise. Some of these learning points are somewhat personal, so I will instead just list what I have learned about living in Europe for the first time, the Swedish life and culture, and what it is like to be a student at Lund University specifically.


### 1. The winter season was sometimes really depressing...
I will get a negative point of living in Sweden out of the way first. I arrived in Lund right in the middle of the winter season. I do not remember exactly how cold it was - all I know is that I was slightly shivering.

"Hey, you doing alright? The cold does not seem to be treating you well."

Here I was, with a few other new international students and student ambassadors on Arrival Day, an orientation day organised by the university for new students to settle in easily in Lund. A student ambassador had asked me that question I could have only expected.

"I am managing, I don't think I am treating the cold well." I laugh.

I think some new students were used to it, because their home countries were probably similar in temperature as well. But as someone who came from Australia when it was summer there and when we had >35°C temperatures there.

Nevertheless, I persevered and accepted the situation. There is this one quote that I do not quite recall who said it, but I repeated this paraphrase of the quote several times throughout my first day in Sweden:

"I cannot control the situation around me, but I can control the way I adapt to the situation."

So I made sure to stay warm with multiple layers, even going so far as to re-use the same warm clothes because I clearly didn't bring enough of them. There is another aspect about the days in winter that I am not a fan of: the days during winter are incredibly dark and short. Essentially, the sun is only out for so long during the day that I pretty much had to rely on daily Vitamin D supplements. As someone who really enjoys summer, safe to say I did not enjoy most of winter.

I also reassured myself:

"It's okay, the wonderful spring and summer seasons will come soon before you know it."

All that aside, I will say there were some winter days that I actually enjoyed. There were some days where it was lightly snowing so the snow on the ground was not deep at all, which made for some aesthetic views. Coming from Australia where we rarely ever get snow, I found myself standing on a path, admiring winter in its beauty. When snow is not slushy or muddy, it can truly be pretty.

As I write this in autumn, I do indeed somewhat miss winter after all but I am only enjoying this moment in fall and I am enthusiastic to see what spring and summer in Sweden is like.


### 2. Sweden is super sustainable!
Well, at least in Lund, the level of sustainability is quite high, and it did not come as a surprise to me when I found out that in [QS World University Rankings for 2026](https://www.topuniversities.com/sustainability-rankings), Lund University was ranked #1 for Sustainability! Frankly, I was not even aware that this was the case prior to arriving at Lund, but I am nevertheless glad to experience it for a few months. Here are some ways of Lund upholding sustainability that I have noticed:

#### Bike-riding is common and encouraged here!
While it makes sense for a small university town like Lund to have lots of people riding bikes (as compared to a much bigger city in Stockholm where I did not see many bikes), bike-riding is insanely common and convenient here. With a bike, I could probably get to anywhere in Lund in the span of at most half an hour. It is also possible to bike from Lund to Malmö in a bit over an hour, but I learned my lesson after riding to and from Lund and Malmö on only my 5th day in Sweden, on a day that was super cold and foggy and dark, simply because I merely wanted to see what the bouldering gym was like.

*yeahh,,, that was a mistake and half alright*

In any case, I have been riding my bike so often that I have even given my bike her own name: Semlala

I remember the day I first met... I found her off a listing on Facebook Marketplace from another exchange student who was leaving because she had just finished her semester. I was on Marketplace because buying second-hand was cheaper and more sustainable than buying a fresh new one.

![Semla](/assets/semlala.png)

This is her. She's a bit rusty but she's beautiful on the inside and has literally been by my side this entire time. Everywhere I go, Semlala goes as well. She even helps me carry my groceries!

Apart from bikes, in Lund there are objectives to limit waste and overconsumption, such as:
- [Circle Centre Lund](https://www.circlecentrelund.org/), a place where people can give their items away to be lent to those who wish to have it
- [Gröna rummet](https://www.afbostader.se/en/stay-with-us/accommodation-information/waste-sorting/bulky-waste/) (which is literally the Green Room in English) by AF Bostäder, also a place where people can give their items away for people to come in to take whatever they please. I managed to find the very desk and chair that I am using at this moment from Gröna rummet!
- multiple waste sorting bin areas like these.
- public transportation is electrified, reducing greenhouse gas emissions
- almost 99% of household waste is then recycled and converted into energy

Apparently, by 2045, Sweden is projected to go completely fossil-free and have 100% renewable energy. Yes, this is quite ambitious, but I do believe that after experiencing first-hand how sustainable Sweden is, that these goals can certainly be accomplished.
