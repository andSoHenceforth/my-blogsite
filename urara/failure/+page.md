Some two weeks ago, I was at a retreat by NanoLund, Lund University's centre for nanoscience and also the largest research envirionment in the field of nanoscience in Sweden. It was quite fun staying at a retreat for 2 days listening to various presentations, networking and eating exquisite 2-course meals, all free of charge!

In particular, one presentation stuck out to me in particular. It was a presentation on "Failure" by an experienced table tennis coach by the name of Magnus Mallander. He gave an engaging presentation on what failure is like and how to deal with failure. What is failure? Who gets to define it? If failure is supposed to be good, why does it feel so bad when it happens?

One key word that I got out of his presentation was this: "*obehag*".

*Obehag* is a Swedish word that in English, means "discomfort" or "unease". It is when you are experience something that brings you distress physically or mentally. In Swedish culture, a person's comfort zone is often related to the *obehag* zone.

To put it simply
- Failure often happens when we try something new. For example, maybe one day I decide to go fishing at a pier where there are plentiful fish, only to go home with none because I have 0 fishing experienc
- *Obehag* is that specific feeling, that prevents me from trying new things. For example, I want to go fishing but I am in *obehag* right now.

Now, this morning is a prime example of *obehag* and failure.

This morning, I received the results for my mathematics exam that I had taken just a few days ago. When I got the notification that the results had released, I immediately checked it out. I thought I did well on the exam. but to my surprise, I had actually failed!

I think this is the first time I had ever failed an exam before, point blank period. 

At first, I tried to excuse myself:
- I am on my exchange semester; I am at this university in a foreign country for the first time, with completely different education standards from what I am used to
- My answers surely might have been marked incorrectly; I felt that even if there were some minor errors, in some questions I thought I had laid out the main logic
- This was the first masters-level course I had ever taken, so no doubt it was harder than bachelors-level courses
- I had been out travelling, which meant skipping classes, which meant missing out on key content

I felt like it wasn't completely fair at first, and I tried shifting the blame onto other things (that were sort of under my control anyways), because it felt better to me.

Even though the above were perfectly valid concerns, after a while I came to realise my own faults:
- Despite this being an exchange semester where my main reason was to embrace Swedish culture and travel around Europe, this still is an academic semester and I actually have to study if I want to pass my courses

And following on from this, I took a deep breath and mentally figured out my next steps from here; it is not the end of the world. I can embrace this *obehag* of not feeling I was good enough, and see what I can do to try to avoid failure from the next time now that I am more familiar with how the exam will be like.

My thinking was as follows: I am able to resit the exam in a few weeks where hopefully I will do much better, or I can try to enrol myself in an easier maths course, or I have to extend another semester at my home university to do one more maths course to make up for the potential failure, which would not be bad at all since most graduate jobs in Australia start at the start of the year.

In my opinion, I think it's important to embrace failure by looking inwards to see what went wrong and then to look outwards to see what I can do to improve in the future. Failure is often looked down upon because it is the opposite of success, and while that is true, it is also true that success cannot exist without failure. It is from failure that lessons are learned, that growth in individuals and groups grow. 

In short, we can find strength and comfort in owning up to the choices that we have made.

May *obehag* drive us to potential future success.
