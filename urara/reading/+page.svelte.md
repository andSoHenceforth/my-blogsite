"It is only with the heart that one can see rightly; what is essential is invisible to the eye" - Antoine de Saint-Exupéry, author of The Little Prince.


Reading books has been a passion of mine since I could learn how to.. well, read. 

In my bedroom, I have a lot of books on my bookshelves, from way back when I was 4 years old to today: novels of more than 500 pages, history books, science books, Tintin volumes, manga, and I still have my children's pictures books. Despite being much older than a toddler, I still enjoy reading my copy of 'The Very Hungry Caterpillar'; it evokes nostalgia like no other.

In primary school and secondary school, I read a lot in the library during rainy days. There were even times when stubborn young me didn't want to play with his friends and just wanted to read!

And now, in university, I don't have as much time or freedom to read. However, I do my best to always try to make time for a bit of reading every day!

In this day and age, technology has evolved to a point where media is consumed online, yet consumed relatively little on books. It sucks because while Google exists and we could access information or media in a blink of an eye, books still hold a lot of value and knowledge that many people tend to miss out on. No matter if it is a fiction or non-fiction book, there are generally quite a few good things to take away from reading a book to its end.

I am curious what the future holds. Will there still be as many books written and book lovers in 50 years time? A 100? Maybe by that time, technology and perhaps even us, as humans, will render books useless as we no longer need to take time to read them nor do we need to read it in the first place as we may already have more than enough knowledge ourselves.

At this time, fortunately or unfortunately we are not up to that point yet. Books still hold sentimental value to me and I enjoy reading whenever I can. Whether between university classes or on the train, I try to make time to read even at least a few sentences.

Although I love to read books of any genre usually without judging their cover, I especially enjoy the genre of science fiction (otherwise known as sci-fi). The intersection of science and reality conjured by the author in such a way that the story feels almost surreal is fascinating to me. Science fiction novels serve as an extension of our daily, normal albeit sometimes boring lives. You would be hard pressed not to catch me daydreaming about being in my own sci-fi story!

One science fiction novel I absolutely loved is actually, in my opinion, an underrated story that needs more love: *The Tunnel to Summer, the Exit of Goodbyes.* by Mei Hachimoku. It is a sci-fi/romance novel with the sci-fi theme being time. There are many books out there with a theme of time, more specifically time travel for the sole purpose of going back and fixing past mistakes. However, this light novel is unique in that instead of time travel, it is more of a time dilation; this is the scientific phenomenon where time appears (or in fact, is) faster or slower to someone. Furthermore, the novel explores past memories and burdens, as well as a clear morale by the end of it. If you would like to know more about this book, please check out my other post here!

On another note, I tend to love books where one of the main characters is someone whose background or values I can relate to on a personal level, because it becomes so much easier for me to really immerse myself in the book and pretend I'm in the setting the characters I'm in. For example: observant and methodical MCs (Hercule Poirot, *Murder on the Orient Express* by Agatha Christie), isolated MCs who are ridiculed or bullied in School (Kokoro and the six other students, *Lonely Castle in the Mirror* by Mizuki Tsujimura) etc.

This has been my experience with books. It is far and away my favourite hobby of all and I am probably the most happiest version of myself when I am allowed to ramble on and on about books!
